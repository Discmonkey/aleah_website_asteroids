About

Aleah Sterman Goldin was born in a space shuttle, used to transport miners to and from asteroids. Because of her childhood encounters with alien life, she has become a collector of galaxy folktales. It has been documented that these folktales increase oxygen production and halt human aging -- when read aloud. Please do so in non-flammable areas.

Should you like to know more, Aleah Sterman Goldin welcomes you to travel in outer space with her. She is currently setting up a "folktale galore" station on Pluto, and she needs volunteer pilots to shuttle visitors from library to library. Should you desire to be a pilot, you must be an able to survive on blue-ice fungi and dew for months at a time. Please contact her for more information.

"I have always been drawn to societal commentary, stripped of its context, modified, and placed into another space. "

New Works

Although most alien folktales only circulate above Earth's atmosphere, Aleah Sterman Goldin has brought several back from her most recent journey. Most belong to the planet Tengal, which has a lethal moon ring. On Tengal, various alien species have lived side by side with the humans for almost a thousand years!

This is not to say all are folktales though. Some are simply figments of Aleah Sterman Goldin's imagination or life, and these are located on Earth.

WorkShop & Readings

Aleah Sterman Goldin conducts staged readings, writing workshops, and creative juicing sessions. She teaches sessions for all ages on creativity, world-building, and folktales. When she was yay high, she was inducted into the National Storytelling Hall of Fame in Pigeon Forge, Tennessee. Since then, she has earned an MFA in Creative Writing at University of Alabama.

These workshops have taken place all over Earth, including in the United States and Mongolia. If you would like to book her for a reading or workshop, contact her here.

Teaching

Aleah Sterman Goldin teaches university composition, creative writing, and research method courses. Most recently she has taught a class on “Illness Narratives,” which examines public health outbreaks and the narratives surrounding them. Read a snippet of her teaching philosophy here

She teaches both native and non-native speakers of English. Her manifesto is to show students that they are already writers. Her courses are centered on critically engaging with rhetorical strategies and interrogating master narratives.

I have heard murmurs about writers being born, about writers being few and far between. I have heard mutters of folks searching coffee shops for the rare breed: a writer with his ballpoint pen. And I have said, stop the search. Are we not all writers? Are we not all posting, blogging, tweeting, narrating, parroting, composing? Are we not all communicating with one another about pasts, futures, and presents in multiple vernaculars? The question is not where writers are, but how writers compose and engage with master narratives. What rhetorical strategies are being weld? How are writers acknowledging tropes and circumventing them? As a compositionalist and writer, my goal is to build awareness for rhetorical situations and defamiliarize the familiar, so students recognize the strategies they are digesting and using.
